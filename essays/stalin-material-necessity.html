Stalin: A Historical and Dialectical Necessity Undone by Khrushchev

By Irfan Khan

Abstract: This essay argues that Joseph Stalin was not an aberration or a deviation from socialism, but a historical necessity produced by the material conditions of the Soviet Union. It analyzes Stalin’s rule not through moral judgment but through dialectical and historical materialism. The USSR emerged from a backward agrarian economy, encircled by hostile capitalist powers, and threatened by internal counterrevolution. Under such conditions, rapid industrialization, agricultural collectivization, and the centralization of political authority were essential to state survival. Stalin’s system functioned as a historical buffer, enabling the USSR to overcome underdevelopment and defeat Nazi Germany in the largest military conflict in history. The essay further argues that Nikita Khrushchev’s de-Stalinization was a premature political retreat that dismantled stabilizing structures before socialism had matured institutionally. This decision not only fractured the USSR internally but also provided the West with ideological ammunition during the Cold War, contributing to the long-term erosion of Soviet legitimacy and eventual collapse in 1991. History produces necessity before morality, and Stalin must be understood within that necessity.

Introduction

History often produces figures who are not morally desirable but structurally necessary. Joseph Stalin was one of them. This essay does not seek to defend Stalin on moral grounds, nor does it justify the repressive aspects of his rule. Instead, it argues from a materialist and dialectical perspective that Stalin was a historical necessity for the survival and consolidation of the Soviet state. The USSR emerged from a backward, agrarian Tsarist empire into a hostile capitalist world system. Under such conditions, industrial acceleration and political centralization were not optional; they were existential. Stalin’s leadership, though brutal, provided the structural buffer required to transform the USSR into a modern industrial power capable of surviving foreign invasion, class struggle, and geopolitical encirclement. The failure to recognize this explains why Nikita Khrushchev’s de-Stalinization was not a triumph of socialism but a premature political retreat that destabilized the Soviet system.

To resume, for such a topic we must understand historical grounding. Marx predicted a revolutionary overthrow of the capitalist order in a developed industrial capitalist society like Britain or Germany—not poor Tsarist Russia. That matters a lot not only in the context of the USSR but also Mao & China, but for now we must focus on the USSR. The primary method of the essay and analysis of such remains dialectical to reason.

Due to the country being massively under-industrialized while entering the Marxist state, it was at a very premature stage. The entire structure of state was at existential risk. The USSR faced invasion threats, economic sabotage, and internal counter-revolution. Harsh central control was necessary for survival.
